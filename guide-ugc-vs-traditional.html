UGC Video Ads vs. Traditional Ads: What DTC Brands Need to Know

Rexran Guides

"UGC-style" ads — content shot to look like it came from a real customer's phone rather than a studio — have become a default part of most DTC ad accounts. That doesn't mean traditional, polished creative is obsolete. It means the two formats do different jobs, and knowing which one to reach for saves budget and testing time.

What makes UGC-style ads different

A UGC-style ad is built to blend into the feed instead of interrupting it. Handheld framing, natural lighting, a person talking directly to camera — visually, it reads more like a recommendation from a friend than an advertisement. That's the entire point: on platforms like Instagram Reels and TikTok, viewers scroll past anything that looks too "produced" before the hook even lands.

This makes UGC-style creative especially strong for cold prospecting, where you're trying to earn a few seconds of attention from someone who has never heard of the brand, and for testing new angles quickly and cheaply before committing to a bigger production.

Where traditional, polished creative still wins

Cinematic or studio-style ads still matter — for brand awareness pushes, product launches, and placements where a rougher look reads as low-quality rather than authentic, like YouTube pre-roll or connected TV. A well-lit, well-composed product film also does something UGC can't: it signals craft and quality directly, which matters for higher-priced products where trust is part of the sell.

The practical answer

Most DTC accounts that perform well don't pick one format — they run both, in rotation, and let performance data decide the mix. A common pattern: test several UGC-style hooks cheaply to find what angle resonates, then invest in a cinematic version once that angle is proven to justify the extra polish.
